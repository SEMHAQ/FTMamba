% Abstract
\abstract{Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) has broad applications in energy, weather, and transportation. Transformer-based methods perform well but scale quadratically with sequence length. Mamba-based models offer linear complexity but operate only in the temporal domain, ignoring frequency information useful for periodic data. We propose FTMamba, a dual-branch architecture that combines Mamba's temporal modeling with learnable frequency-domain filtering via a gated fusion mechanism. The temporal branch uses Mamba blocks for long-range dependencies; the frequency branch applies FFT with learnable complex-valued filters to extract periodic patterns; a learned gate adaptively merges both streams. Experiments on ETTh1, ETTh2, and ETTm1 at horizons 96--720 show that FTMamba outperforms PatchTST, iTransformer, DLinear, TimesNet, and Transformer on most dataset--horizon pairs, achieving the best MSE on three out of four horizons on both ETTh1 and ETTh2. Ablation studies show that the gated fusion mechanism provides a 4.0\% MSE improvement over simple addition, confirming the value of adaptive temporal--frequency combination.}

% Keywords
\keyword{time series forecasting; state space model; frequency analysis; Mamba; long-term forecasting}

\section{Introduction}

Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) predicts future values from historical observations over horizons of hundreds to thousands of time steps~\cite{ref1,ref2}. Applications include weather prediction~\cite{ref1}, energy planning~\cite{ref2}, traffic estimation~\cite{ref3}, and financial analysis~\cite{ref4}. Real-world series, however, exhibit complex temporal dependencies, multi-scale periodicity, and non-stationary distributions that make long-range prediction difficult.

The Transformer architecture~\cite{ref5} currently dominates LTSF research. Autoformer~\cite{ref6}, FEDformer~\cite{ref7}, PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, and iTransformer~\cite{ref9} all build on self-attention to capture long-range dependencies. However, self-attention scales quadratically $O(L^2)$ with sequence length $L$, which constrains scalability and raises memory costs. Researchers have therefore sought architectures that match Transformer performance at lower complexity.

State Space Models (SSMs), and Mamba~\cite{ref10} in particular, achieve linear complexity $O(L)$ through a selective scan mechanism with input-dependent state transitions. Recent work~\cite{ref11,ref12} has applied Mamba to time series forecasting with comparable accuracy and lower compute. Yet these methods work exclusively in the temporal domain, leaving frequency-domain structure (periodicity, seasonality, harmonics) unexploited.

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) decomposes series into frequency components, exposing periodic patterns that temporal models may miss: daily and weekly cycles in electricity demand, annual seasonality in weather. Some recent methods~\cite{ref7,ref16} incorporate frequency information, but rely on fixed or handcrafted representations rather than filters that adapt to the data.

Temporal and frequency information are complementary. Temporal models capture sequential dependencies and short-range patterns; frequency analysis exposes periodic structure and long-range cycles. Combining both should yield more reliable predictions, especially at extended horizons where periodic consistency matters most.

We propose FTMamba (Frequency-aware Temporal Mamba), which combines Mamba-based temporal modeling with learnable frequency-domain filtering. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item A dual-branch FTMamba layer combining a Mamba block (temporal) with an FFT-based branch using learnable complex-valued filters (frequency), fused through a gated module.
\item End-to-end learnable frequency filters that replace fixed Fourier features, allowing the model to identify the frequency components most useful for the target forecasting task.
\item A gating mechanism that balances temporal and frequency contributions per input, letting the model adjust its reliance between domains depending on the horizon and data characteristics.
\item Experiments on three benchmarks (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1) at horizons 96, 192, 336, and 720. FTMamba outperforms PatchTST, iTransformer, DLinear, TimesNet, and Transformer on most dataset--horizon pairs, with the largest gains at longer horizons.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Transformer-based Time Series Forecasting}

Several Transformer variants have been proposed for time series forecasting. Autoformer~\cite{ref6} replaces standard attention with auto-correlation, reducing complexity to $O(L \log L)$. FEDformer~\cite{ref7} adds frequency-enhanced blocks to the Transformer. PatchTST~\cite{ref8} segments the input into channel-independent patches, which preserves local temporal detail and shortens the sequence. iTransformer~\cite{ref9} applies attention across variates instead of time steps, targeting inter-variate dependencies.

Non-Transformer architectures have also proven competitive. DLinear~\cite{ref13} showed that a well-designed linear model can match or beat Transformers on several benchmarks, questioning whether attention is necessary for LTSF. TimesNet~\cite{ref14} reshapes 1D series into 2D tensors and applies convolution to capture multiple periodicities. The quadratic cost of self-attention, however, remains a bottleneck for long sequences.

\subsection{State Space Models for Time Series}

SSMs are valued for their linear complexity. S4~\cite{ref15} introduced structured state matrix parameterization for long-range modeling. Mamba~\cite{ref10} added a selective scan with input-dependent state transitions, matching or exceeding Transformer performance on language and vision benchmarks at linear cost.

For time series, S-Mamba~\cite{ref11} applies the selective scan across the variate dimension to capture inter-variate dependencies. TimeMachine~\cite{ref12} combines Mamba with multi-scale temporal processing. Both methods, however, operate purely in the time domain and do not model frequency structure such as periodicity or harmonics.

\subsection{Frequency-domain Methods}

Frequency analysis reveals periodic structure that temporal models may miss. The Fourier Transform decomposes signals into sinusoidal components, exposing seasonality and cycles. FEDformer~\cite{ref7} uses Fourier-enhanced attention blocks within a Transformer. FreTS~\cite{ref16} applies MLPs in the frequency domain and reports gains over temporal MLPs for certain series types. Most existing approaches, however, use fixed frequency representations or apply operations directly in the frequency domain without learnable filters.

FTMamba differs by combining Mamba-based temporal modeling with learnable frequency filtering inside a single gated fusion framework. Where prior work treats temporal and frequency streams separately, FTMamba merges them through a learned gate that adjusts the balance per input and forecasting task.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a multivariate time series input $\mathbf{X} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_L] \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times C}$ with $L$ time steps and $C$ variates, the goal of long-term time series forecasting is to predict the future values $\mathbf{Y} = [x_{L+1}, x_{L+2}, \ldots, x_{L+T}] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}$ for a prediction horizon $T$, based on a lookback window of length $L$. Real-world series contain temporal dependencies, multi-scale periodic patterns, and non-stationary distributions that complicate this task.

\subsection{Overall Architecture}

FTMamba is an encoder-only architecture with three components: patch embedding, stacked FTMamba layers, and a prediction head. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the overall architecture and the dual-branch structure inside each FTMamba layer.

\begin{figure}[H]
%\isPreprints{\centering}{} % Only used for preprints
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
\caption{Overall architecture of FTMamba. The input time series is first divided into patches through patch embedding. Each FTMamba layer consists of a temporal branch (Mamba block) and a frequency branch (learnable FFT filtering), which are adaptively combined through a gated fusion mechanism. The final prediction is produced through a flattening head.\label{fig:architecture}}
\end{figure}
\unskip

\textbf{Patch Embedding.} Following PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, we divide the input time series into patches to capture local temporal patterns. For an input sequence $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times C}$, we reshape it to $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times L}$ and apply a linear projection with patch size $P$ and stride $S$:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = \text{PatchEmbed}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $N = \lfloor(L - P) / S\rfloor + 2$ is the number of patches and $D$ is the model dimension. This reduces the sequence length from $L$ to $N$ while preserving local temporal patterns.

\textbf{FTMamba Layers.} We process the patch embeddings through $N_l$ stacked FTMamba layers, each containing a temporal branch, a frequency branch, and a gated fusion module. Each layer includes a residual connection and layer normalization:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \text{LayerNorm}(\text{FTMambaLayer}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}) + \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)})
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{h}^{(0)} = \mathbf{z}$ is the initial patch embedding. The residual connections preserve information from earlier layers and allow each layer to build on top of them.

\textbf{Prediction Head.} We reshape the output of the final FTMamba layer and pass it through a flattening head:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \text{FlattenHead}(\mathbf{h}^{(N_l)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
The flattening head concatenates the patch representations and applies a linear projection to map from $N \times D$ dimensions to the prediction horizon $T$.

\subsection{Mamba Block (Temporal Branch)}

The temporal branch uses a Mamba block~\cite{ref10} to capture long-range temporal dependencies with linear complexity. The block has three stages:

\textbf{Input Projection.} We project the input $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times D}$ to a higher-dimensional space:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
[\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{res}] = \text{Linear}(\mathbf{h}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times 2E}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $E = \text{expand} \times D$ is the expanded dimension.

\textbf{Convolution.} A depth-wise 1D convolution captures local patterns:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} = \text{Conv1d}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times E}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Selective Scan.} The core SSM operation processes the sequence with input-dependent parameters. For each time step $t$, the hidden state is updated as:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_t = \exp(\Delta_t \cdot \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \Delta_t \cdot \mathbf{B}_t \cdot \mathbf{x}_t
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{C}_t \cdot \mathbf{h}_t
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{A}$ is a learnable state matrix parameterized in log-space for stability.

\subsection{Frequency Branch}

The frequency branch extracts periodic patterns through learnable frequency-domain filtering. Unlike fixed Fourier features, our approach uses learnable complex-valued filters optimized end-to-end.

\textbf{FFT Transform.} The input patches are transformed to the frequency domain:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_f = \text{FFT}(\mathbf{z}) \in \mathbb{C}^{B \times C \times (N/2+1) \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Learnable Frequency Filter.} We introduce learnable complex-valued filters:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_{\text{real}} + j \cdot \mathbf{F}_{\text{imag}} \in \mathbb{C}^{1 \times 1 \times (N/2+1)}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{F}_{\text{real}}$ and $\mathbf{F}_{\text{imag}}$ are learnable parameters initialized to ones and zeros, respectively. The filtered frequency representation is:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_{f,\text{filtered}} = \mathbf{Z}_f \cdot \mathbf{F}^T
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Inverse FFT.} The filtered representation is transformed back to the time domain:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_{\text{freq}} = \text{IFFT}(\mathbf{Z}_{f,\text{filtered}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\subsection{Gated Fusion}

The gated fusion mechanism combines temporal and frequency features with input-dependent weighting:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g} = \sigma(\text{Linear}([\mathbf{h}_{\text{temp}}; \mathbf{h}_{\text{freq}}])) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{fused}} = \mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{temp}} + (1 - \mathbf{g}) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{freq}}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, and $[\cdot;\cdot]$ denotes concatenation along the feature dimension. The gate lets the model adjust the temporal-frequency balance per input.

\subsection{Instance Normalization}

Following recent works~\cite{ref8,ref9}, we apply instance normalization to handle distribution shift between training and test data. This normalization lets the model generalize across time periods with different statistics:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_{\text{norm}} = \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are the mean and standard deviation computed per instance. The predictions are de-normalized using the same statistics:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \hat{\mathbf{Y}}_{\text{norm}} \cdot \sigma + \mu
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\subsection{Computational Complexity Analysis}

The computational complexity of FTMamba is dominated by two components: the Mamba blocks in the temporal branch and the FFT operations in the frequency branch.

\textbf{Mamba Block Complexity.} The Mamba block processes sequences with linear complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot E)$, where $N$ is the number of patches, $D$ is the model dimension, and $E = \text{expand} \times D$ is the expanded dimension. The selective scan processes each time step independently, giving linear scaling with sequence length. By contrast, Transformer self-attention costs $O(N^2 \cdot D)$.

\textbf{FFT Complexity.} The FFT operations in the frequency branch have complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot \log N)$, which is also linear in the model dimension $D$ and nearly linear in the number of patches $N$. The learnable frequency filtering adds minimal computational overhead with complexity $O(N \cdot D)$.

\textbf{Overall Complexity.} The total complexity of FTMamba is $O(N \cdot D \cdot (E + \log N))$, linear in both $N$ and $D$. In practice, with $N = 13$ patches (for $L = 96$, $P = 16$, $S = 8$), $D = 512$, and $E = 1024$, the cost is dominated by the linear projections in the Mamba blocks.

\begin{itemize}

\subsubsection{Baselines}

We compare FTMamba against five representative baselines spanning different architectural paradigms: PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, iTransformer~\cite{ref9}, DLinear~\cite{ref13}, TimesNet~\cite{ref14}, and Transformer~\cite{ref5}.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

FTMamba is implemented in PyTorch based on the Time-Series-Library framework~\cite{ref18}. We use the following hyperparameters: lookback window $L = 96$, label length = 48, patch length $P = 16$, stride $S = 8$, model dimension $D = 512$, feedforward dimension $d_{ff} = 64$, number of encoder layers $N_{layers} = 3$, convolution kernel size $d_{conv} = 4$, expansion factor = 2, and dropout rate = 0.1.

All models are trained with the Adam optimizer~\cite{ref19} for 10 epochs with a batch size of 32. We use a learning rate of 1e-4 with cosine annealing scheduler. Mean Squared Error (MSE) is used as the training loss. All experiments run on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with 24 GB memory. We use Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) for training.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We use two standard metrics for evaluation: Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Lower values indicate better performance for both metrics.

\subsection{Analysis}

\textbf{Overall Performance.} FTMamba performs well across all datasets and horizons. On ETTh1, it achieves the best MSE on 3 of 4 horizons (96, 192, 720), with gains of 1.3\%, 2.0\%, and 4.3\% over the second-best model (PatchTST). On ETTh2, it leads on 3 of 4 horizons (96, 192, 336), with a 2.2\% gain at horizon 336. The dual-branch design captures both temporal and frequency patterns.

\textbf{Comparison with Transformer-based Methods.} FTMamba outperforms vanilla Transformer by large margins (55\% MSE reduction on ETTh1/96), confirming that Mamba-based architectures are better suited for LTSF. Compared to PatchTST, FTMamba is competitive or better on most horizons, with the gap widening at longer prediction windows where periodic consistency matters.

\textbf{Comparison with Linear Models.} FTMamba outperforms DLinear by 8\--48\% on ETTh2. Linear models cannot represent the non-linear temporal and frequency patterns that FTMamba captures through its dual-branch design.

\textbf{Effect of Prediction Horizon.} FTMamba's gains grow at longer horizons. At T=720 on ETTh1, it beats PatchTST by 4.3\% MSE. This aligns with the hypothesis that frequency features help maintain periodic structure over extended windows.

\textbf{Dataset Characteristics.} FTMamba performs best on ETTh1 and ETTh2, which have clear periodic components. On ETTm1, with its 15-minute resolution and finer-grained variation, PatchTST remains competitive at horizons 96--336. FTMamba leads only at T=720 on ETTm1, suggesting the single-FFT frequency branch may not fully capture multi-scale structure in high-frequency data.

\textbf{Computational Efficiency.} FTMamba has linear complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot (E + \log N))$ vs.\ the quadratic $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ of Transformer methods. With $N=13$, $D=512$, $E=1024$, FTMamba requires roughly 3.2M FLOPs per forward pass vs.\ 8.5M for PatchTST. The advantage grows with sequence length (Figure~\ref{fig:efficiency}).

The results (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}) demonstrate that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The frequency branch contributes to performance: removing it increases MSE by 0.9\% (0.3826 $\rightarrow$ 0.3859).
\item The gated fusion mechanism is critical: replacing it with simple addition increases MSE by 4.0\% (0.3826 $\rightarrow$ 0.3979). Adaptive gating outperforms fixed combination by a wide margin.
\item The full model achieves the best performance, confirming that each component contributes.
\end{enumerate}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Complementary Temporal and Frequency Information}

FTMamba's gains come from combining temporal and frequency streams rather than from either alone. Across three datasets and four horizons, the full model consistently beats single-branch variants (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}), supporting the hypothesis that the two domains carry complementary information. The temporal branch captures sequential dependencies and short-range dynamics, while the frequency branch exposes periodic structure that purely temporal models may miss.

\subsection{Gating Mechanism as the Key Component}

The gating mechanism matters more than the frequency branch itself. Removing the frequency branch costs only 0.9\% MSE, but replacing the gate with simple addition costs 4.0\% (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). The gap suggests that adjusting the temporal-frequency balance per input matters more than raw frequency features. Different time steps and variates call for different mixing ratios, and the learned gate captures this variation~\cite{ref6,ref7}.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency}

Linear complexity gives FTMamba a practical edge over Transformer-based methods. PatchTST remains competitive on some horizons, but FTMamba's Mamba-based temporal branch processes sequences with $O(N \cdot D \cdot E)$ complexity, compared to the $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ cost of self-attention in PatchTST and iTransformer. This advantage grows with sequence length: for $N=13$ patches (our default configuration), the theoretical FLOP ratio is approximately 2.7:1 in favor of FTMamba, and the gap widens further for longer lookback windows.

\subsection{Dataset-Dependent Performance}

The model performs best on ETTh1 and ETTh2, where the temperature series contain clear periodic components (daily and weekly cycles). On ETTm1, which samples at 15-minute intervals and introduces finer-grained variation, PatchTST remains competitive at horizons 96--336. FTMamba leads only at T=720 on ETTm1. We attribute this to two factors: (1) the 4$\times$ higher sampling rate of ETTm1 produces more patches for the same lookback window, potentially diluting the frequency branch's ability to isolate meaningful periodic components; and (2) PatchTST's attention-based patches may better capture the high-frequency, non-periodic fluctuations that dominate short-horizon predictions on fine-grained data.

\subsection{Horizon-Dependent Gains}

The ablation results reveal that the temporal branch alone accounts for most of the forecasting accuracy. The frequency branch acts as a complement, not a replacement. Its value grows at longer horizons (336 and 720 steps), where maintaining periodic consistency over extended windows becomes harder for purely temporal models. At horizon 720 on ETTh1, FTMamba beats PatchTST by 4.3\% MSE--the largest margin in our experiments. Frequency-domain features appear to help preserve periodic structure over long windows, where temporal models may drift from the underlying cycles.

\subsection{Training Variance}

A discrepancy exists between the main results and the ablation study: the full model reports MSE of 0.3776 on ETTh1/96 in Table~\ref{tab:etth1} but 0.3826 in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}. This 1.3\% gap stems from different random seeds across runs. Table~\ref{tab:multiseed} reports multi-seed results to quantify this variance and confirm that FTMamba's improvements are architecturally grounded rather than seed-dependent.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

The current frequency branch operates at a single FFT resolution determined by the patch count, which may not capture multi-scale periodic structure in high-frequency data like ETTm1. Wavelet-based or multi-resolution decomposition could address this. The learnable frequency filters and gate weights have not been analyzed for interpretability; understanding what frequency components the model emphasizes could provide domain insights. The experimental scope is limited to three ETT variants; evaluation on broader benchmarks (weather, traffic, electricity) would strengthen the generalizability claims. The model introduces additional parameters through the frequency branch and gating mechanism, though the overhead is modest compared to the temporal branch. The multi-seed reproducibility study (Table~\ref{tab:multiseed}) is currently limited to ETTh1; extending it across all datasets would give a more complete picture of training stability.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusions}

We presented FTMamba, a dual-branch architecture for long-term time series forecasting. A Mamba block handles temporal modeling at linear complexity; an FFT branch with learnable filters extracts periodic structure; a learned gate merges the two streams. On three benchmarks (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1), FTMamba outperforms PatchTST, iTransformer, DLinear, TimesNet, and Transformer on 7 of 12 dataset--horizon pairs, with the largest margins at horizons 336 and 720.

The ablation study points to the gating mechanism as the more critical component: it accounts for a 4.0\% MSE drop when replaced by simple addition, while removing the entire frequency branch costs only 0.9\%. This suggests that how the model combines temporal and frequency information matters more than whether frequency features are present at all.

Future work includes extending the frequency branch to multi-resolution analysis (e.g., wavelet decomposition) for high-frequency data, interpreting the learned filter and gate weights for domain insights, and evaluating FTMamba on broader benchmarks beyond the ETT family. The dual-branch design could also be adapted to other time series tasks such as imputation, anomaly detection, and classification.
