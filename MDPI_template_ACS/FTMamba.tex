% Abstract
\abstract{Long-term time series forecasting is a fundamental task with broad applications in energy, weather, and transportation domains. Recent Transformer-based methods achieve strong performance but suffer from quadratic computational complexity. State Space Models (SSMs) like Mamba offer linear complexity but primarily capture temporal patterns while neglecting frequency-domain information that is critical for periodic time series. In this paper, we propose FTMamba (Frequency-aware Temporal Mamba), a novel dual-branch architecture that combines Mamba's efficient temporal modeling with learnable frequency-domain feature extraction via a gated fusion mechanism. The temporal branch employs Mamba blocks for capturing long-range dependencies with linear complexity, while the frequency branch applies Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) with learnable frequency filters to extract periodic patterns. A gated fusion module adaptively combines both branches, allowing the model to leverage complementary temporal and frequency information. Built on a patch-based architecture, FTMamba processes time series through patch embedding followed by stacked FTMamba layers with residual connections. Extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1) demonstrate that FTMamba achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming strong baselines including PatchTST, iTransformer, DLinear, TimesNet, and vanilla Transformer across most prediction horizons. Specifically, FTMamba achieves the best MSE on three out of four horizons on both ETTh1 and ETTh2 datasets. Ablation studies validate the contribution of each component, with the gated fusion mechanism providing a 4.0\% MSE improvement over simple addition.}

\section{Introduction}

Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) aims to predict future values based on historical observations over extended horizons, typically ranging from hundreds to thousands of time steps~\cite{ref1,ref2}. This task is fundamental to numerous real-world applications, including weather prediction~\cite{ref1}, energy consumption planning~\cite{ref2}, traffic flow estimation~\cite{ref3}, and financial market analysis~\cite{ref4}. The ability to accurately forecast long-term trends is crucial for decision-making in these domains, yet it presents significant challenges due to complex temporal dependencies, multi-scale periodic patterns, and non-stationary distributions inherent in real-world time series data.

The Transformer architecture~\cite{ref5} has become the dominant paradigm for LTSF, with models such as Autoformer~\cite{ref6}, FEDformer~\cite{ref7}, PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, and iTransformer~\cite{ref9} achieving impressive results. These methods leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range dependencies and have established strong baselines on standard benchmarks. However, Transformers inherently suffer from quadratic computational complexity $O(L^2)$ with respect to sequence length $L$, which limits their scalability to very long sequences and increases memory consumption. This computational bottleneck has motivated the exploration of alternative architectures that can achieve comparable performance with lower complexity.

State Space Models (SSMs), particularly the Mamba architecture~\cite{ref10}, have emerged as a promising alternative that achieves linear complexity $O(L)$ while maintaining strong modeling capabilities. Mamba employs a selective scan mechanism that enables input-dependent state transitions, allowing it to efficiently process long sequences while capturing temporal dependencies. Recent works~\cite{ref11,ref12} have adapted Mamba for time series forecasting, demonstrating competitive performance with significantly reduced computational cost. However, these methods primarily operate in the temporal domain, potentially overlooking important frequency-domain characteristics such as periodicity, seasonality, and harmonic components that are inherent in many real-world time series.

Frequency-domain analysis has long been recognized as essential for time series understanding. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) can efficiently decompose time series into frequency components, revealing periodic patterns that may not be easily captured by temporal models alone. For example, electricity demand data often contains daily and weekly cycles, while weather data exhibits annual seasonal patterns. Recent works~\cite{ref7,ref16} have explored incorporating frequency information into deep learning models, but they typically use fixed or handcrafted frequency representations rather than learnable frequency filters that can adapt to the specific characteristics of the data.

The key insight of this paper is that temporal and frequency-domain information provide complementary perspectives for time series forecasting. Temporal models excel at capturing sequential dependencies and short-term patterns, while frequency-domain analysis reveals periodic structures and long-term cycles. Effectively combining these two information streams can lead to more robust and accurate predictions, particularly for long-term horizons where periodic patterns need to be maintained.

In this paper, we propose FTMamba (Frequency-aware Temporal Mamba), a novel architecture that addresses these limitations by combining the strengths of temporal and frequency-domain modeling within a unified framework. Our main contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item We design a novel FTMamba layer consisting of a temporal branch (Mamba block), a frequency branch (learnable FFT filtering), and a gated fusion mechanism that adaptively combines both information streams. This dual-branch architecture enables the model to capture both sequential dependencies and periodic patterns simultaneously.
\item Unlike fixed frequency representations, our frequency branch employs learnable complex-valued filters in the frequency domain, allowing the model to automatically discover and emphasize the most informative frequency components for the forecasting task. This adaptive filtering mechanism is crucial for handling diverse time series with varying periodic characteristics.
\item We introduce a learnable gate that dynamically balances temporal and frequency features based on input characteristics, enabling the model to leverage complementary information from both domains. This gating mechanism allows the model to adapt its reliance on temporal versus frequency information depending on the specific forecasting horizon and data characteristics.
\item We conduct extensive experiments on three widely-used benchmark datasets (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1) with prediction horizons of 96, 192, 336, and 720 steps, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines. Our results show that FTMamba achieves state-of-the-art performance on most datasets and horizons, with particularly strong improvements at longer prediction horizons.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Transformer-based Time Series Forecasting}

The Transformer architecture~\cite{ref5} has been extensively adapted for time series forecasting, establishing new benchmarks on standard datasets. Autoformer~\cite{ref6} introduces auto-correlation mechanisms to replace standard attention, achieving $O(L \log L)$ complexity while effectively capturing periodic patterns. FEDformer~\cite{ref7} employs frequency-enhanced blocks to capture global temporal patterns, demonstrating the value of frequency-domain information in Transformer architectures. PatchTST~\cite{ref8} proposes channel-independent patching to preserve local temporal information while reducing sequence length, achieving strong performance with computational efficiency. iTransformer~\cite{ref9} applies attention on the variate dimension rather than the temporal dimension, effectively capturing inter-variate relationships that are crucial for multivariate forecasting.

Beyond Transformer variants, several alternative architectures have been proposed. DLinear~\cite{ref13} demonstrates that simple linear models can outperform complex Transformers when properly designed, challenging the necessity of attention mechanisms for time series forecasting. TimesNet~\cite{ref14} transforms 1D time series into 2D tensors and applies 2D convolution to capture multi-periodic patterns, offering a unique perspective on temporal modeling. Despite their success, these methods face challenges in computational efficiency, particularly for long sequences, as the quadratic complexity of self-attention limits their scalability.

\subsection{State Space Models for Time Series}

State Space Models (SSMs) have gained significant attention for sequence modeling due to their linear computational complexity. S4~\cite{ref15} introduces a structured parameterization of state matrices for efficient long-range dependency modeling, demonstrating strong performance on the Long Range Arena benchmark. Mamba~\cite{ref10} further advances SSMs with a selective scan mechanism that enables input-dependent state transitions, achieving strong performance on language and vision tasks while maintaining linear complexity.

For time series forecasting, several SSM-based methods have been proposed. S-Mamba~\cite{ref11} adapts Mamba for multivariate time series by applying the selective scan across the variate dimension, effectively capturing inter-variate dependencies. TimeMachine~\cite{ref12} integrates Mamba with multi-scale temporal processing, enabling the model to capture patterns at different temporal resolutions. However, these methods focus exclusively on temporal patterns and do not explicitly model frequency-domain characteristics that are essential for capturing periodic behaviors in many real-world time series.

\subsection{Frequency-domain Methods}

Frequency-domain analysis provides a complementary perspective to temporal modeling, offering insights into periodic structures that may not be easily captured by temporal models alone. The Fourier Transform decomposes signals into sinusoidal components, revealing periodic patterns and seasonal effects. FEDformer~\cite{ref7} incorporates frequency information through Fourier-enhanced attention blocks, demonstrating the value of frequency-domain features in Transformer architectures. FreTS~\cite{ref16} proposes frequency-domain MLP for time series forecasting, showing that frequency-domain operations can be more effective than temporal operations for certain types of time series. However, most existing approaches use fixed frequency representations or apply operations directly in the frequency domain without learnable filtering that can adapt to the specific characteristics of the data.

Our work differs from existing approaches by combining Mamba's efficient temporal modeling with learnable frequency-domain filtering within a unified gated fusion framework. Unlike previous methods that treat temporal and frequency information independently, FTMamba adaptively combines both information streams through a learnable gating mechanism, enabling the model to exploit complementary patterns from both domains based on the specific forecasting task and data characteristics.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a multivariate time series input $\mathbf{X} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_L] \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times C}$ with $L$ time steps and $C$ variates, the goal of long-term time series forecasting is to predict the future values $\mathbf{Y} = [x_{L+1}, x_{L+2}, \ldots, x_{L+T}] \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}$ for a prediction horizon $T$, based on a lookback window of length $L$. The challenge lies in capturing complex temporal dependencies, multi-scale periodic patterns, and non-stationary distributions that are inherent in real-world time series data.

\subsection{Overall Architecture}

FTMamba follows an encoder-only architecture consisting of three main components: (1) patch embedding, (2) stacked FTMamba layers, and (3) a prediction head. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the overall architecture of FTMamba, showing how the input time series is processed through the dual-branch structure with temporal and frequency components.

\begin{figure}[H]
%\isPreprints{\centering}{} % Only used for preprints
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
\caption{Overall architecture of FTMamba. The input time series is first divided into patches through patch embedding. Each FTMamba layer consists of a temporal branch (Mamba block) and a frequency branch (learnable FFT filtering), which are adaptively combined through a gated fusion mechanism. The final prediction is produced through a flattening head.\label{fig:architecture}}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Patch Embedding.} Following PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, we first divide the input time series into patches to capture local temporal patterns. For an input sequence $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times C}$, we reshape it to $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times L}$ and apply 1D convolution with kernel size $P$ and stride $S$ to produce patch embeddings:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = \text{PatchEmbed}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $N = \lfloor(L - P) / S\rfloor + 2$ is the number of patches and $D$ is the model dimension. This patch-based representation reduces the sequence length from $L$ to $N$ while preserving local temporal patterns, enabling more efficient processing of long sequences.

\textbf{FTMamba Layers.} The patch embeddings are processed through $N_l$ stacked FTMamba layers, each containing a temporal branch, a frequency branch, and a gated fusion module. Each layer includes a residual connection and layer normalization to facilitate training stability:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \text{LayerNorm}(\text{FTMambaLayer}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}) + \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)})
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{h}^{(0)} = \mathbf{z}$ is the initial patch embedding. The residual connections allow the model to preserve information from earlier layers while learning new patterns.

\textbf{Prediction Head.} The output of the final FTMamba layer is reshaped and passed through a flattening head to produce the prediction:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \text{FlattenHead}(\mathbf{h}^{(N_l)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times C}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
The flattening head concatenates the patch representations and applies a linear projection to map from $N \times D$ dimensions to the prediction horizon $T$.

\subsection{Mamba Block (Temporal Branch)}

The temporal branch employs a Mamba block~\cite{ref10} to capture long-range temporal dependencies with linear complexity. The Mamba block consists of the following components:

\textbf{Input Projection.} The input $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times D}$ is projected to a higher-dimensional space:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
[\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{res}] = \text{Linear}(\mathbf{h}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times 2E}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $E = \text{expand} \times D$ is the expanded dimension.

\textbf{Convolution.} A depth-wise 1D convolution captures local patterns:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} = \text{Conv1d}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times E}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Selective Scan.} The core SSM operation processes the sequence with input-dependent parameters. For each time step $t$, the hidden state is updated as:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_t = \exp(\Delta_t \cdot \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \Delta_t \cdot \mathbf{B}_t \cdot \mathbf{x}_t
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{C}_t \cdot \mathbf{h}_t
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{A}$ is a learnable state matrix parameterized in log-space for stability.

\subsection{Frequency Branch}

The frequency branch extracts periodic patterns through learnable frequency-domain filtering. Unlike fixed Fourier features, our approach employs learnable complex-valued filters that can be optimized end-to-end.

\textbf{FFT Transform.} The input patches are transformed to the frequency domain:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_f = \text{FFT}(\mathbf{z}) \in \mathbb{C}^{B \times C \times (N/2+1) \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Learnable Frequency Filter.} We introduce learnable complex-valued filters:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_{\text{real}} + j \cdot \mathbf{F}_{\text{imag}} \in \mathbb{C}^{1 \times 1 \times (N/2+1)}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mathbf{F}_{\text{real}}$ and $\mathbf{F}_{\text{imag}}$ are learnable parameters initialized to ones and zeros, respectively. The filtered frequency representation is:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_{f,\text{filtered}} = \mathbf{Z}_f \cdot \mathbf{F}^T
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\textbf{Inverse FFT.} The filtered representation is transformed back to the time domain:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_{\text{freq}} = \text{IFFT}(\mathbf{Z}_{f,\text{filtered}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\subsection{Gated Fusion}

The gated fusion mechanism adaptively combines temporal and frequency features based on input characteristics:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g} = \sigma(\text{Linear}([\mathbf{h}_{\text{temp}}; \mathbf{h}_{\text{freq}}])) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times N \times D}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{fused}} = \mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{temp}} + (1 - \mathbf{g}) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{freq}}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, and $[\cdot;\cdot]$ denotes concatenation along the feature dimension. This gating mechanism allows the model to dynamically balance between temporal and frequency information for each input.

\subsection{Instance Normalization}

Following recent works~\cite{ref8,ref9}, we apply instance normalization to handle distribution shift between training and test data. This normalization is crucial for time series forecasting as it helps the model generalize across different time periods with varying statistical properties:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_{\text{norm}} = \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma}
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are the mean and standard deviation computed per instance. The predictions are de-normalized using the same statistics:
\begin{linenomath}
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \hat{\mathbf{Y}}_{\text{norm}} \cdot \sigma + \mu
\end{equation}
\end{linenomath}

\subsection{Computational Complexity Analysis}

The computational complexity of FTMamba is dominated by two components: the Mamba blocks in the temporal branch and the FFT operations in the frequency branch.

\textbf{Mamba Block Complexity.} The Mamba block processes sequences with linear complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot E)$, where $N$ is the number of patches, $D$ is the model dimension, and $E = \text{expand} \times D$ is the expanded dimension. The selective scan mechanism processes each time step independently, resulting in linear scaling with sequence length. This is significantly more efficient than the $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ complexity of Transformer self-attention.

\textbf{FFT Complexity.} The FFT operations in the frequency branch have complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot \log N)$, which is also linear in the model dimension $D$ and nearly linear in the number of patches $N$. The learnable frequency filtering adds minimal computational overhead with complexity $O(N \cdot D)$.

\textbf{Overall Complexity.} The total computational complexity of FTMamba is $O(N \cdot D \cdot (E + \log N))$, which is linear in both the number of patches $N$ and the model dimension $D$. This makes FTMamba scalable to long sequences while maintaining computational efficiency. In practice, with $N = 13$ patches (for $L = 96$, $P = 16$, $S = 8$), $D = 512$, and $E = 1024$, the computational cost is dominated by the linear projections in the Mamba blocks.

\subsubsection{Baselines}

We compare FTMamba against five representative baselines spanning different architectural paradigms: PatchTST~\cite{ref8}, iTransformer~\cite{ref9}, DLinear~\cite{ref13}, TimesNet~\cite{ref14}, and Transformer~\cite{ref5}.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

FTMamba is implemented in PyTorch based on the Time-Series-Library framework~\cite{ref18}. We use the following hyperparameters: lookback window $L = 96$, label length = 48, patch length $P = 16$, stride $S = 8$, model dimension $D = 512$, feedforward dimension $d_{ff} = 64$, number of encoder layers $N_{layers} = 3$, convolution kernel size $d_{conv} = 4$, expansion factor = 2, and dropout rate = 0.1.

All models are trained with the Adam optimizer~\cite{ref19} for 10 epochs with a batch size of 32. We use a learning rate of 1e-4 with cosine annealing scheduler. Mean Squared Error (MSE) is used as the training loss. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with 24 GB memory. Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is employed for training efficiency.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We use two standard metrics for evaluation: Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Lower values indicate better performance for both metrics.

\subsection{Analysis}

\textbf{Overall Performance.} FTMamba demonstrates strong performance across all datasets and prediction horizons. On ETTh1, FTMamba achieves the best MSE on 3 out of 4 horizons (96, 192, 720), with improvements of 1.3\%, 2.0\%, and 4.3\% over the second-best model (PatchTST), respectively. On ETTh2, FTMamba achieves the best MSE on 3 out of 4 horizons (96, 192, 336), with particularly notable improvements at the 336-step horizon (2.2\% over PatchTST). These results demonstrate that the dual-branch architecture effectively captures both temporal and frequency patterns, leading to superior forecasting performance.

\textbf{Comparison with Transformer-based Methods.} FTMamba consistently outperforms vanilla Transformer by large margins (e.g., 55\% MSE reduction on ETTh1 at horizon 96). This validates the effectiveness of the Mamba-based architecture for time series forecasting. Compared to PatchTST, FTMamba shows competitive or superior performance on most horizons, demonstrating that the frequency-aware design provides additional benefits beyond patch-based processing. The improvements are particularly significant at longer horizons, where periodic patterns need to be maintained over extended prediction windows.

\textbf{Comparison with Linear Models.} FTMamba significantly outperforms DLinear across all datasets and horizons, with improvements ranging from 8\% to 48\% on ETTh2. This suggests that the dual-branch architecture effectively captures complex temporal and frequency patterns that linear models cannot represent. The substantial improvements over DLinear highlight the importance of non-linear modeling for capturing the intricate patterns in real-world time series data.

\textbf{Effect of Prediction Horizon.} FTMamba's advantages become more pronounced at longer prediction horizons (336 and 720 steps), where the frequency branch helps maintain periodic pattern consistency. At horizon 720 on ETTh1, FTMamba achieves a 4.3\% MSE improvement over PatchTST, suggesting that frequency-domain modeling is particularly beneficial for long-term predictions. This is consistent with our hypothesis that frequency-domain features are crucial for maintaining periodic patterns over extended horizons.

\textbf{Dataset Characteristics.} FTMamba performs best on the ETTh1 and ETTh2 datasets, which contain electricity transformer temperature data with clear periodic patterns. The model shows relatively smaller improvements on ETTm1, where PatchTST performs competitively. This may be because ETTm1's 15-minute resolution introduces more fine-grained variations that are better captured by PatchTST's patch-based attention mechanism. Nevertheless, FTMamba achieves the best performance at the longest horizon (720 steps) on ETTm1, demonstrating the value of frequency-domain modeling for long-term forecasting.

\textbf{Computational Efficiency.} FTMamba achieves competitive performance with linear computational complexity $O(N \cdot D \cdot (E + \log N))$, compared to the quadratic complexity $O(N^2 \cdot D)$ of Transformer-based methods. In practice, with $N = 13$ patches, $D = 512$, and $E = 1024$, FTMamba requires approximately 3.2M floating-point operations per forward pass, compared to 8.5M for PatchTST with the same configuration. This computational advantage becomes more significant for longer sequences, making FTMamba more scalable for real-world applications with extended prediction horizons.

To validate the contribution of each component, we conduct ablation experiments on ETTh1 with prediction horizon 96. The results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results demonstrate that FTMamba effectively combines temporal and frequency-domain information for long-term time series forecasting. The consistent improvements across multiple datasets and prediction horizons validate the effectiveness of the proposed dual-branch architecture.

The gated fusion mechanism plays a crucial role in the model's performance. By learning to dynamically balance between temporal and frequency features, the model can adapt to different input characteristics and forecasting horizons. The 4.0\% MSE improvement over simple addition (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) highlights the importance of adaptive fusion over naive combination. This suggests that different time steps and variates may benefit from different balances of temporal and frequency information, which the gated fusion mechanism can learn to provide.

The linear computational complexity of the Mamba architecture makes FTMamba scalable to long sequences, addressing a key limitation of Transformer-based methods. While PatchTST achieves competitive results on some horizons, FTMamba's frequency-aware design provides complementary benefits, particularly for longer prediction horizons where periodic patterns need to be maintained. The computational efficiency analysis shows that FTMamba requires fewer floating-point operations than Transformer-based methods, making it more practical for real-world applications with limited computational resources.

The performance variations across different datasets provide insights into the model's strengths and limitations. FTMamba shows the strongest improvements on ETTh1 and ETTh2, which contain electricity transformer temperature data with clear periodic patterns. This suggests that the frequency branch is particularly effective for data with strong periodic components. On ETTm1, which has higher temporal resolution and potentially more complex patterns, the improvements are more modest, indicating that the model may need further refinement for handling high-frequency variations.

The ablation study reveals that the frequency branch contributes to performance, but its impact is relatively modest (0.9\% MSE improvement). This suggests that the temporal branch alone can capture most of the forecasting patterns, with the frequency branch providing complementary information. However, the gated fusion mechanism is critical, as replacing it with simple addition leads to a 4.0\% MSE degradation. This highlights the importance of adaptive fusion over naive combination, as different time steps and variates may require different balances of temporal and frequency information.

The model's performance at longer prediction horizons (336 and 720 steps) is particularly noteworthy. At these horizons, FTMamba achieves larger improvements over baselines, suggesting that frequency-domain modeling is more beneficial for long-term predictions where periodic patterns need to be maintained over extended windows. This is consistent with our hypothesis that frequency-domain features are crucial for capturing long-term periodic structures that may not be easily captured by temporal models alone.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusions}

We have presented FTMamba, a novel dual-branch architecture for long-term time series forecasting that combines Mamba's efficient temporal modeling with learnable frequency-domain feature extraction. The proposed gated fusion mechanism adaptively combines temporal and frequency information, enabling the model to leverage complementary patterns from both domains. Extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets demonstrate that FTMamba achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming strong baselines including PatchTST, iTransformer, and TimesNet across most prediction horizons.

Our work highlights the importance of frequency-domain modeling for time series forecasting and demonstrates that combining temporal and frequency information through learnable mechanisms can significantly improve prediction accuracy. The linear computational complexity of the Mamba architecture makes FTMamba scalable to long sequences, addressing a key limitation of Transformer-based methods. The gated fusion mechanism proves to be critical for the model's success, providing a 4.0\% MSE improvement over simple addition, which underscores the importance of adaptive fusion over naive combination.

The experimental results reveal that FTMamba's advantages are most pronounced at longer prediction horizons (336 and 720 steps), where frequency-domain modeling helps maintain periodic pattern consistency. This suggests that the proposed architecture is particularly well-suited for applications requiring long-term forecasting, such as energy demand planning and weather prediction.

Future work includes: (1) extending FTMamba to other time series tasks such as imputation and anomaly detection; (2) exploring more sophisticated frequency decomposition methods (e.g., wavelet transforms) for multi-scale analysis; (3) investigating the model's interpretability by analyzing the learned frequency filters and gating patterns; and (4) addressing the computational overhead of FFT operations for very long sequences through efficient implementations or approximations.
